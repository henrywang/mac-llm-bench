ARTICLE 1: Apple Unveils M5 Ultra Chip With 32-Core CPU

Apple on Monday announced the M5 Ultra, its most powerful chip to date, featuring a 32-core CPU,
80-core GPU, and up to 192GB of unified memory. The chip is built using TSMC's second-generation
3-nanometer process and connects two M5 Max dies using Apple's UltraFusion interconnect fabric,
achieving memory bandwidth of 800GB/s. Apple claims the M5 Ultra delivers up to 2.4x the
machine learning performance of the M3 Ultra, driven largely by a new 64-core Neural Engine.
The chip will appear first in the new Mac Pro and Mac Studio configurations shipping next month,
with prices starting at $3,999 and $1,999 respectively. Developers working with large AI models
locally have been the primary target audience, with Apple emphasizing the chip's ability to run
600-billion-parameter models without splitting them across machines.

ARTICLE 2: OpenAI Raises $10B at $300B Valuation in Latest Funding Round

OpenAI closed a $10 billion funding round on Tuesday, pushing its valuation to $300 billion,
making it the most valuable private company in the world. The round was led by SoftBank, with
participation from existing investors Microsoft and Thrive Capital. The capital will be used
primarily to expand compute infrastructure, including the buildout of additional data centers
in the United States and Japan under the Stargate initiative. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman said
the funds will allow the company to accelerate the development of its next-generation models
and expand access to its products in emerging markets. The deal follows OpenAI's transition
to a for-profit public benefit corporation structure completed earlier this year, which removed
the cap on investor returns that had previously limited fundraising flexibility.

ARTICLE 3: EU Passes AI Liability Directive, Requiring Companies to Prove Fault

The European Parliament voted 412 to 89 on Wednesday to pass the AI Liability Directive,
a landmark piece of legislation that shifts the burden of proof in AI-related harm cases.
Under the new rules, companies deploying high-risk AI systems — including those used in hiring,
credit scoring, and medical diagnosis — must proactively disclose training data, model behavior
logs, and risk assessments when a claimant alleges harm. If a company refuses or is unable to
provide this documentation, courts may presume negligence. The directive applies to any company
offering AI services to EU residents, regardless of where the company is headquartered, meaning
US and Asian tech firms will need to comply. Companies have 24 months to achieve compliance.
Industry groups including TechEU criticized the directive as creating excessive documentation
burdens that could stifle innovation, while consumer advocates praised it as a necessary step
toward AI accountability.
